FCS Teaching Topics: Group Work - Top Tips

Take-Aways

Methods That Work in Creating Successful Groups

  • Give students a short form or survey - include options to list their individual skills and strengths (see the Group Formation form link below. Thanks to Dr. Tami Meredith for sharing)
  • Create groups based on students' skills and strengths
  • Give each individual a role in the group - i.e., leader/manager, lead programmer, technical writer, researcher etc. (Ensure women and people who are perceived to be female are not relegated to purely document writing or presenting - they also need an opportunity to code!)
  • Include peer feedback - students must evaluate the performance of others in the group
  • (Added as suggestion - students should be able to highlight to the instructor if they feel strongly about NOT working with a particular student)

Factors That Inhibit Group Work

  • Poorly designed tasks without clear goals and outcomes
  • Lack of clear roles for students
  • Poor communication among group members

Group work - not all methods are the same

Group work (also known as cooperative learning) is a valuable method of instruction that can be implemented in classes to allow students to work together to produce projects, research papers etc. It supports cooperation and collaboration among individuals for a common purpose.
There are many methods to formulating groups:
  • students can create their own groups
  • instructors can group students to generate a balance of genders, ethnicities, cultures, etc.
  • instructors can create groups according to students abilities
  • instructors can create groups according to skill and strengths
All the above methods can be effective if implemented correctly, however, research suggests that instructor created groups are the most effective in developing new trusting relationships, an important interpersonal skill. Most students believe that student created groups are more effective, but research suggests this simply is not true (Hilton and Philips, 2010).

Some Advice for your Students BEFORE they Begin their Project

Know What You Want
Talk with your teammates about the quality of work your team aspires to produce and the amount of commitment to reach that goal. What type of team do you want to be? Define the general rules by which the team will work. How will the team determine who does what? How often will you meet, and for how long? How will you keep in touch between meetings? Spending a few minutes at the start to put these "rules" in writing can save you much frustration and disappointment later.
Plan How to Get There
Team projects can feel overwhelming at first, and you may be tempted to quickly split-up the work so that everyone can get started on it. Do not do this. Take time to identify:
  • The specific steps needed to complete the project
  • The skills required at each step
  • The strengths of each team member. Two benefits of being in a team are that you do not have to be an expert in everything and you do not have to do it all yourself. So, assign work to team members based on their strengths and workload, ensuring that everyone gets to do their fair share. Research shows that teams earn better grades when everyone participates in a meaningful way
Work as a Team
Teams are more than just groups of people. To be a team, you must be committed to the same goal and be willing to “step up” when you can. Teams can accomplish much when working as a single unit, but not without the individual effort made by team members. Research shows that the highest project grades are earned when team members work together bringing their skills and abilities to bear on each task.
Build the Team
Teams rarely are perfect when they first start. It takes time to build the trust that is needed when relying on others. Help each other along by scheduling times to review each other’s work and to give constructive feedback on how it can be improved. As a team, assess the whole team’s performance and the openness of its communications. Do this often, and small difficulties will be less likely to turn into big problems.
